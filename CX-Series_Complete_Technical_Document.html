Hemostasis & Coagulation: Background

Primary vs Secondary Hemostasis

Primary Hemostasis (White Clot): Platelet-based hemostasis - Vascular constriction, platelet adhesion and activation. Common in arterial thrombosis. Drugs: Aspirin, Clopidogrel
Secondary Hemostasis (Red Clot): Fibrin-based hemostasis - Coagulation cascade leading to fibrin clot formation. Common in venous thrombosis. Drugs: Heparin, Warfarin

Coagulation Cascade: Three Pathways

The coagulation cascade is organized into three pathways that converge to form a fibrin clot:

1. Extrinsic Pathway (PT Test)

Initiated by tissue damage and Tissue Factor (TF/Factor III). Simple pathway: TF + VII → X → II (Thrombin) → Fibrin clot. Detected by PT test. Vitamin K dependent factors: II, VII, IX, X.

2. Intrinsic Pathway (APPT Test)

Initiated by contact activators (kaolin, silica, ellagic acid) and phospholipid. Complex pathway with 6-7 steps: XII → XI → IX + VIII (AMPLIFICATION) → X → II → Fibrin clot. Includes amplification cascade with multiple factor interactions.

3. Common Pathway (FIB/TT Test)

Final converging pathway: Factor X → II (Thrombin) → Fibrin clot formation. Tests measure fibrin clot formation time. Factor XIII stabilizes the cross-linked fibrin clot.

Test Parameters: Principles & Applications

1. PT (Prothrombin Time)

Principle

PT measures the extrinsic coagulation pathway and measures the time to clot formation after tissue factor (TF) and calcium ions are added to plasma. Pathway: Tissue damage → TF + Factor VII → Factor X → Prothrombin (II) → Thrombin → Fibrin clot

Detection Method

CX-Series uses optical transmission method (turbidimetric/kinetic detection). The analyzer measures optical density change as fibrin forms.

Clinical Applications

  • Monitoring warfarin anticoagulation therapy (Target INR: 2.0-3.0 for most conditions; 2.5-3.5 for mechanical heart valves)
  • Screening for vitamin K deficiency
  • Evaluating liver function and synthetic capacity
  • Assessing extrinsic coagulation pathway (Factors VII, X, II, V, Fibrinogen)
  • Pre-operative bleeding risk assessment
  • Monitoring DIC (Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation)

Normal Range & Critical Values

  • PT: 11-13.5 seconds (varies by reagent)
  • INR: 0.8-1.1 (normal) | 2.0-3.0 (therapeutic range for warfarin)

2. APPT (Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time)

Principle

APPT measures the intrinsic coagulation pathway (endogenous pathway). Platelet-depleted plasma is incubated at 37°C with phospholipids and contact activators (kaolin, silica, or ellagic acid), then calcium ions are added to initiate clot formation.

Pathway: Contact activator + Phospholipid → Factor XII → XI → IX + VIII (AMPLIFICATION) → X → II → Fibrin clot

Why APPT is Complex

  • Contains 6-7 cascade steps vs PT's 3-4 steps
  • Includes amplification cascade (Factor IX + VIII acting as bottleneck)
  • Multiple factor interactions and dependencies
  • Sensitive to heparin, lupus anticoagulant, and factor inhibitors
  • Requires advanced kinetic analysis for accurate results

Detection Method

CX-Series uses optical transmission method with kinetic analysis. Response curve analysis enables detection of subtle kinetic changes in the intrinsic pathway.

Clinical Applications

  • Monitoring heparin (unfractionated and low-molecular-weight) anticoagulation therapy
  • Screening for endogenous coagulation factor deficiencies
  • Detecting lupus anticoagulant and other inhibitors
  • Evaluating intrinsic pathway (Factors XII, XI, IX, VIII, X, II, V, Fibrinogen)
  • Routine pre-operative bleeding risk assessment
  • Diagnosing hemophilia A (Factor VIII deficiency) and hemophilia B (Factor IX deficiency)
  • Investigating prolonged bleeding time
  • Monitoring patients with DIC

Normal Range & Critical Values

  • APPT: 28-38 seconds (varies by reagent)
  • Therapeutic (on Heparin): 1.5-2.5× baseline

APPT Mixing Study (Detailed)

Purpose: Differentiate between coagulation factor deficiency and inhibitor (anticoagulant, antibody, or Lupus anticoagulant)

Principle

When prolonged APPT is detected, a 1:1 mixing test is performed with Normal Pooled Plasma (NPP). If deficiency exists, mixed plasma corrects to normal (factor activity ≥50%). If inhibitor exists, mixed plasma remains prolonged (inhibitor affects normal factors too).

CX-9000 7-Step Automatic Mixing Test

  1. Patient plasma loaded and tested (APPT1)
  2. Prepare Normal Pooled Plasma (NPP)
  3. Mix Patient Plasma + NPP (1:1 ratio) - Immediate correction test
  4. Test mixed plasma immediately (APPT2, APPT3)
  5. Calculate Correction Index: RI_CT = [(APPT3-APPT2)/APPT1]×100%
  6. Incubate mixed plasma at 37°C for 120 minutes (Time/Temperature dependency check)
  7. Test incubated mixed plasma (APPT6, APPT7) to detect time-temperature dependent inhibitors

Interpretation Table

3. FIB (Fibrinogen - Clauss Method)

Principle

Fibrinogen measures the time to fibrin clot formation after direct thrombin addition. High-concentration thrombin is added, bypassing upstream factors to directly measure fibrinogen functionality. Measurement: Time for fibrin clot formation is inverse to fibrinogen concentration.

Clinical Applications

  • Assessment of fibrinogen deficiency (congenital or acquired)
  • Evaluation of patients with bleeding tendency
  • Monitoring DIC (Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation)
  • Pre-operative bleeding risk assessment
  • Identifying dysfibrinogenemia (abnormal fibrinogen function)

Normal Range

  • Fibrinogen: 2.0-4.0 g/L (Reference range)
  • Elevated: Inflammation, acute phase response, infection
  • Decreased: DIC, liver disease, hypofibrinogenemia

4. TT (Thrombin Time)

Principle

Thrombin Time measures the time to fibrin clot formation after direct thrombin addition. Similar to Fibrinogen test but provides functional assessment of fibrinogen quality and detection of thrombin inhibitors.

Clinical Applications

  • Assessing fibrinogen function and concentration
  • Detecting heparin presence (prolonged TT)
  • Identifying dysfibrinogenemia
  • Evaluating patients on anticoagulant therapy

Normal Range

Thrombin Time: 14-21 seconds (varies by reagent)

5. D-Dimer (Immunoturbidimetry Method)

Principle

D-Dimer measures fibrin degradation products (crosslinked fibrin fragments). Elevated in conditions with increased fibrin formation and breakdown (thrombosis, DIC, sepsis). Highly sensitive but low specificity for thromboembolism (high NPV for ruling out disease).

Clinical Applications

  • Ruling out Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) and Pulmonary Embolism (PE) - High Negative Predictive Value
  • Detecting Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC)
  • Monitoring hypercoagulable states
  • Post-operative thrombosis monitoring

Normal Range

D-Dimer: <0.5 mg/L (FEU) or <250 ng/mL

6. FDP (Fibrin Degradation Products - Immunoturbidimetry)

Principle

FDP measures fibrinogen and fibrin breakdown products. More sensitive than D-Dimer for detecting fibrinolysis. Elevated in DIC, thrombosis, and active fibrinolysis.

Clinical Applications

  • Detecting Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC) - More sensitive than D-Dimer
  • Monitoring active fibrinolysis
  • Assessing coagulation/fibrinolysis imbalance

7. AT-III (Antithrombin III - Chromogenic Substrate Method)

Principle

Antithrombin III (AT-III) is a natural anticoagulant that inhibits thrombin and other coagulation factors. CX-Series uses chromogenic substrate method for precise AT-III activity measurement.

Clinical Applications

  • Evaluating natural anticoagulation reserve
  • Detecting AT-III deficiency (congenital or acquired)
  • Assessing heparin responsiveness (Heparin requires AT-III for anticoagulant effect)
  • Monitoring patients with thrombosis risk
  • Identifying heparin resistance (low AT-III leads to reduced heparin efficacy)

Normal Range

  • AT-III Activity: 80-120% (Reference range)
  • Low AT-III: Increased thrombosis risk, may require higher heparin doses

Sample Quality Validation: HIL Detection

Overview

CX-Series provides automatic sample pre-detection for sample integrity. The system automatically flags and rejects samples with optical interference.

H: Hemolysis Detection

  • Cause: Red blood cell breakdown releases hemoglobin
  • Impact: Red color interferes with optical detection, invalidating results
  • Action: Automatic flag and rejection

I: Icterus Detection

  • Cause: High bilirubin levels (Jaundice/Hyperbilirubinemia)
  • Impact: Yellow color interferes with optical signal
  • Action: Automatic flag and rejection

L: Lipemia Detection

  • Cause: High triglyceride levels (lipid particles in plasma)
  • Impact: Increases background turbidity, causes false clotting signal
  • Action: Automatic flag and rejection

Additional Pre-Detection

CX-Series also detects: Sample volume insufficiency
