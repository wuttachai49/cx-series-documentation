🔬 CX-Series Detection Methods & Wavelengths

Optical Detection Technology for Coagulation Testing

📊 Overview: Optical Detection Systems

CX-Series analyzers use turbidimetric/kinetic optical detection to measure clot formation. As fibrin monomer polymerizes into a clot, light absorbance changes, which the analyzer detects in real-time using multiple wavelengths.

CX-6000 vs CX-9000 Wavelength Specifications

CX-6000 Optical Channels

  • 405 nm (Violet): Primary coagulation testing wavelength
  • 575 nm (Yellow-Green): Secondary measurement & reference
  • 660 nm (Red): Alternative wavelength for specific assays

CX-9000 Optical Channels (Enhanced)

  • 405 nm (Violet): Primary coagulation testing wavelength
  • 545 nm (Green): Optimized secondary measurement
  • 660 nm (Red): Extended range measurement
  • 800 nm (Near-Infrared): Advanced optical flexibility (CX-9000 exclusive)
CX-9000 Advantage: The 800nm infrared channel provides superior optical clarity for hemolytic, icteric, or lipemic samples — a clinical advantage competitors lack.

🎯 Three Optical Detection Methods

Method 1: Threshold Detection

How it works: The analyzer detects when light absorbance crosses a specific threshold value during clot formation.

What it measures: Time until absorbance change reaches a preset threshold (faster, simpler)

Best for: Quick screening tests, high-throughput protocols

Sensitivity: Moderate - detects obvious clot formation

Used in: PT (some protocols), rapid screening assays

Method 2: 1st Derivative (Rate) Detection

How it works: Calculates the rate of change (dA/dt) of light absorbance over time

What it measures: Maximum speed of absorbance change during fibrin polymerization

Best for: More sensitive detection of early fibrin formation

Sensitivity: High - catches subtle changes in clot kinetics

Used in: APPT, Fibrinogen, Thrombin Time (most coagulation tests)

Advantage: More reliable than threshold, fewer false results

Method 3: 2nd Derivative (Acceleration) Detection

How it works: Analyzes the acceleration/deceleration (d²A/dt²) of absorbance change

What it measures: The inflection point where fibrin polymerization peaks

Best for: Most sensitive detection of clot formation onset

Sensitivity: Very High - detects earliest fibrin formation

Used in: APPT (optimal), specialized clotting studies

Advantage: Minimizes interference from background turbidity, most accurate kinetics

🧪 CX-Series Parameters: Detection Methods & Wavelengths

1. PT (Prothrombin Time)

Clinical Significance: Extrinsic pathway + common pathway (4 factors: VII → X → V → II)

🎨 Detection Method

Primary Method: 1st Derivative (Rate Detection)

Alternative: Threshold Detection (for rapid results)

CX-6000 Wavelengths:
405 nm (primary) + 575 nm (reference)
CX-9000 Wavelengths:
405 nm (primary) + 545 nm (optimized secondary)

How It Works

  • Patient plasma + Tissue Factor (TF) + Calcium → triggers extrinsic pathway
  • Analyzer monitors light absorbance at 405nm (violet light)
  • Fibrin polymer formation increases optical density (turbidity)
  • 1st Derivative detects peak rate of change = PT result (10-13 sec normal)
  • 575nm used as optical reference to correct for sample interference

Why Multiple Wavelengths?

405nm is ideal for coagulation (optimal fibrin absorbance). 575nm helps eliminate interference from hemolysis or other optical artifacts.

2. APPT (Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time)

Clinical Significance: Intrinsic pathway + common pathway (7 factors: XII → XI → IX → X → V → II)

🎨 Detection Method

Primary Method: 2nd Derivative (Acceleration Detection) ⭐ MOST SENSITIVE

Why 2nd Derivative: APPT is most sensitive to mild clotting defects. 2nd derivative catches the earliest fibrin formation signals.

CX-6000 Wavelengths:
405 nm (primary) + 660 nm (extended range)
CX-9000 Wavelengths:
405 nm (primary) + 545 nm (optimized) + 800 nm (NIR for hemolytic samples)

How It Works

  • Patient plasma + Partial Thromboplastin (phospholipid) + Activator (kaolin) → intrinsic pathway
  • Analyzer monitors 405nm light absorbance continuously
  • 2nd Derivative detects inflection point = earliest fibrin polymerization = APPT (25-35 sec normal)
  • CX-9000's 800nm infrared provides enhanced detection in difficult samples (hemolytic, icteric, lipemic)

CX-9000 Exclusive Advantage

The 800nm near-infrared channel allows APPT testing in hemolytic samples where competitors fail. This is a significant clinical advantage.

3. Fibrinogen

Clinical Significance: Measures clotting factor I (2-4 g/L normal)

🎨 Detection Method

Primary Method: 1st Derivative (Rate Detection)

Why: Fibrinogen has unique kinetics - measures rate of thrombin-fibrin interaction

CX-6000 & CX-9000:
405 nm (primary) with optical reference wavelengths

How It Works

  • Patient plasma + Thrombin (directly activates fibrinogen) → fibrin polymerization
  • No cascade pathway - pure fibrinogen-to-fibrin conversion
  • Analyzer monitors 405nm absorbance rate change
  • Speed of absorbance increase = fibrinogen concentration
  • Slower rate = low fibrinogen (DIC, liver disease, dilution)

Special Note

Fibrinogen testing is more straightforward than PT/APPT because it bypasses the entire coagulation cascade. Direct thrombin addition means simpler kinetics = more reliable 1st derivative detection.

4. Thrombin Time (TT)

Clinical Significance: Measures fibrinogen quality & quantity + direct thrombin inhibitors

🎨 Detection Method

Primary Method: 1st Derivative (Rate Detection)

Rationale: Similar to fibrinogen - direct thrombin measurement

CX-6000 & CX-9000:
405 nm (primary measurement wavelength)

How It Works

  • Patient plasma + Thrombin reagent → fibrin formation
  • Measures time (or rate) of fibrin polymerization
  • Detects fibrinogen abnormalities (dysfibrinogenemia)
  • Detects direct thrombin inhibitors (dabigatran)
  • 405nm detects fibrin clot formation kinetics (11-16 sec normal)

5. D-Dimer

Clinical Significance: Fibrin degradation marker (rule out thromboembolism)

🎨 Detection Method

Primary Method: Immunoturbidimetric with 1st Derivative

Why Different: D-Dimer uses monoclonal antibodies that bind to D-Dimer epitopes

CX-6000 & CX-9000:
405 nm + optical reference wavelengths

How It Works

  • Patient plasma + D-Dimer specific antibodies (magnetic particles)
  • Antibody-D-Dimer complex causes visible light absorbance change
  • Analyzer measures 405nm absorbance increase = agglutination reaction
  • 1st Derivative detects rate of particle agglutination
  • Concentration proportional to D-Dimer level (< 0.5 µg/mL normal)

Special Note: Immunoassay Method

Unlike PT/APPT/Fibrinogen which are coagulation cascade tests, D-Dimer is an immunoassay. It still uses turbidimetric detection at 405nm, but measures antibody-antigen binding instead of enzymatic clot formation.

6. FDP (Fibrin Degradation Products)

Clinical Significance: Markers of active fibrinolysis (DIC, sepsis, liver disease)

🎨 Detection Method

Primary Method: Immunoturbidimetric with 1st Derivative

Similar to: D-Dimer (immunoassay approach)

CX-6000 & CX-9000:
405 nm + optical reference

How It Works

  • Patient plasma + FDP-specific antibodies (reagent particles)
  • Plasmin breaks down fibrin → releases D-dimer, E-dimer, X-dimer, Y-dimer fragments
  • Antibodies bind these fragments → visible agglutination
  • 405nm measures agglutination turbidity increase
  • 1st Derivative detects particle aggregation rate
  • FDP level indicates active fibrinolysis (< 5 µg/mL normal)

Clinical Context

FDP is less specific than D-Dimer but can be elevated in active bleeding/clotting states. CX-9000's multiple wavelengths help distinguish true FDP elevation from optical interference.

7. AT-III (Antithrombin III)

Clinical Significance: Natural anticoagulant protein (80-120% normal, <60% suggests thrombosis risk)

🎨 Detection Method

Primary Method: Chromogenic substrate with 1st Derivative

Why Different: AT-III is measured via thrombin inhibition kinetics, not fibrin formation

CX-6000 & CX-9000:
405 nm (colorimetric detection of substrate cleavage)

How It Works

  • Patient plasma + Thrombin + Chromogenic substrate (pNA-labeled)
  • AT-III binds to thrombin → inactivates it
  • Uninhibited thrombin cleaves chromogenic substrate → yellow color (pNA release)
  • More AT-III = more thrombin inactivated = LESS color change = LOWER absorbance
  • 405nm detects yellow chromogenic product concentration (inverse relationship)
  • 1st Derivative measures rate of color development (substrate cleavage rate)

Important Principle

Inverse measurement: Unlike other tests, AT-III is measured by ABSENCE of color development. Low AT-III = MORE thrombin activity = MORE color = HIGHER absorbance.

🌈 Wavelength Spectrum Explanation

UV | VIOLET | BLUE | GREEN | YELLOW | ORANGE | RED | NIR

Why 405nm (Violet) for Coagulation?

  • Fibrin absorption peak: 405nm is at the optimal absorbance wavelength for fibrin clots
  • Optimal signal: Provides strongest optical contrast between plasma and fibrin
  • Reduced scatter: Shorter wavelengths (UV/violet) minimize light scattering from RBCs & platelets
  • Clinical standard: All automated coagulation analyzers use ~405nm as primary

Secondary Wavelengths Purpose

  • 545-575nm (Green-Yellow): Reference channel to detect sample interference (hemolysis, lipemia, icterus)
  • 660nm (Red): Extended range measurement, alternative for difficult samples
  • 800nm (NIR - CX-9000 only): Near-infrared provides enhanced penetration in hemolytic samples
CX-9000 Competitive Advantage: The 800nm near-infrared channel is NOT available on competitors. This allows CX-9000 to test hemolytic samples (high bilirubin, high hemoglobin) that would produce invalid results on other analyzers. This is a clinical selling point.
