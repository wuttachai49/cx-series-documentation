🧪 Seven Comprehensive Tests - Detailed Principles

One system. Seven critical tests. Each explained in detail:

1️⃣ PT (Prothrombin Time) Extrinsic Pathway

What It Measures: How quickly blood clots using the extrinsic (tissue factor) pathway
Biochemical Cascade:
Tissue Damage → Tissue Factor (TF) + Factor VII → Factor X → Prothrombin (II) → Thrombin → Fibrin Clot

Why PT Is Important:

  • Warfarin Monitoring: Warfarin blocks Factors II, VII, IX, X (vitamin K dependent). PT increases → INR increases → dose adjustment needed
  • Liver Function: Liver produces most coagulation factors. PT prolongation suggests liver disease
  • Vitamin K Status: Quick detection of vitamin K deficiency (malabsorption, antibiotics)
  • Pre-operative Screening: Identifies bleeding risk before surgery
Normal Range: 11-13.5 seconds (varies by reagent)
Critical Value: Usually >2× normal or critical for patient
INR (Therapeutic): 2.0-3.0 (warfarin) or 2.5-3.5 (mechanical valve)
Clinical Applications:
  • Monitoring patients on Warfarin (anticoagulation)
  • Assessing liver synthetic function
  • Detecting vitamin K deficiency
  • Pre-operative bleeding risk
  • Monitoring liver disease

2️⃣ APPT (Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time) Intrinsic Pathway

What It Measures: How quickly blood clots using the intrinsic (contact activation) pathway - the most complex pathway
Biochemical Cascade (Complex 6-7 Steps):
Contact Activator (Kaolin/Silica) + Phospholipid → Factor XII → XI → IX + VIII (AMPLIFICATION STEP) → X → II (Thrombin) → Fibrin Clot

Why APPT Is Critical:

  • Heparin Monitoring: Heparin enhances Antithrombin which inhibits Factors II, X, IX → APPT increases. Tight therapeutic window (1.5-2.5× baseline)
  • Factor Deficiencies: Detects low Factor VIII (Hemophilia A), Factor IX (Hemophilia B), Factor XI, Factor XII
  • Inhibitor Detection: Lupus anticoagulant, Factor VIII antibodies show as prolonged APPT that doesn't correct with mixing
  • Complex Cascade: 6-7 steps mean many failure points - needs advanced kinetic analysis to detect all abnormalities
Normal Range: 28-38 seconds (varies by reagent)
Therapeutic (Heparin): 1.5-2.5× patient baseline
Criticality: Narrow therapeutic window - requires precision
Clinical Applications:
  • Monitoring heparin therapy (narrow window)
  • Detecting hemophilia A & B (factor deficiencies)
  • Identifying Lupus anticoagulant
  • Detecting Factor VIII inhibitor antibodies
  • Screening for intrinsic pathway disorders
  • Mixing study for differential diagnosis

3️⃣ FIB (Fibrinogen) Common Pathway

What It Measures: The amount of fibrinogen (functional protein that forms clot) using Clauss method - direct thrombin addition
Test Procedure:
Direct Thrombin Addition → Bypasses all upstream factors → Measures only fibrin clot formation time

Why Fibrinogen Is Critical:

  • Bleeding Risk: Low fibrinogen (hypofibrinogenemia) = high bleeding risk. <2g/L = severe bleeding risk
  • DIC Detection: DIC = Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation - fibrinogen consumed rapidly, levels drop dangerously
  • Inflammatory Marker: Elevated fibrinogen indicates acute phase response (infection, inflammation, surgery stress)
  • Quality vs Quantity: Measures functional fibrinogen - some patients have dysfibrinogenemia (abnormal function despite normal quantity)
Normal Range: 2.0-4.0 g/L
Critical Low: <1.0 g/L = Severe bleeding risk
Elevated Significance: Inflammation, infection, acute phase
Clinical Applications:
  • Assessing bleeding risk
  • Detecting DIC (fibrinogen consumed)
  • Monitoring liver disease (liver produces fibrinogen)
  • Identifying hypofibrinogenemia
  • Detecting dysfibrinogenemia (abnormal function)
  • Post-op monitoring

4️⃣ TT (Thrombin Time) Fibrin Formation

What It Measures: Time to fibrin clot formation after direct thrombin addition - tests the final step only
Test Procedure:
Direct Thrombin (high concentration) → Fibrinogen → Fibrin Clot Formation Time

Why Thrombin Time Is Useful:

  • Heparin Detection: Heparin directly interferes with thrombin → Prolongs TT. Quick screening for heparin contamination
  • Fibrinogen Function: Tests functional fibrinogen quality specifically (not just quantity like fibrinogen test)
  • Dysfibrinogenemia: Abnormal fibrinogen molecules can't form proper clots despite normal quantity
  • Anticoagulant Drugs: Detects direct thrombin inhibitors (dabigatran) - prolongs TT
Normal Range: 14-21 seconds (varies by reagent)
Prolonged Significance: Heparin, direct inhibitors, dysfibrinogen
Clinical Use: Heparin screening & confirmation
Clinical Applications:
  • Detecting heparin presence/contamination
  • Assessing fibrinogen function quality
  • Identifying dysfibrinogenemia
  • Detecting direct thrombin inhibitors
  • Confirming prolonged PT/APPT cause

5️⃣ D-Dimer Fibrin Breakdown

What It Measures: Breakdown products of fibrin clots - detects active fibrinolysis and thrombosis
Biological Process:
Fibrin Clot Forms → Plasmin Breaks It Down → D-Dimer Released Into Blood

Why D-Dimer Matters:

  • Thrombosis Screening: D-Dimer ELEVATED = Suggests blood clot (DVT, PE, thrombosis). But elevated D-Dimer has many causes (infection, cancer, post-op)
  • High Negative Predictive Value (NPV): NORMAL D-Dimer = 95% chance NO thrombosis. Very useful for ruling OUT disease
  • DIC Indicator: In DIC, both clotting AND breakdown increase → D-Dimer very high
  • Fibrinolysis Marker: Indicates active clot breakdown - normal in healthy people, elevated with active clotting
Normal Range: <0.5 mg/L (FEU) or <250 ng/mL
Elevated Means: Active clot OR recent clotting event
Rule-Out Test: Normal = 95% NPV for PE/DVT
Clinical Applications:
  • Ruling out DVT/PE (high NPV)
  • Detecting active thrombosis
  • DIC screening
  • Post-operative monitoring
  • Sepsis assessment
  • Cancer-related thrombosis screening

6️⃣ FDP (Fibrin Degradation Products) Fibrinolysis Marker

What It Measures: Early breakdown products of BOTH fibrinogen AND fibrin - more sensitive than D-Dimer
Biological Process:
Plasmin Breaks Down Fibrinogen & Fibrin → Multiple Fragment Sizes (X, D, E, DD, DDD) → FDP Released

Why FDP Is Superior to D-Dimer:

  • More Sensitive: Detects breakdown of BOTH fibrinogen (before clotting) AND fibrin (after clotting) - D-Dimer only sees fibrin fragments
  • DIC Gold Standard: In DIC, FDP often more elevated than D-Dimer because both fibrinogen breakdown AND fibrin breakdown occurring
  • Early Detection: Can show fibrinolysis activation earlier than D-Dimer
  • Acute Fibrinolysis: Primary fibrinolysis (bleeding with clot breakdown) vs secondary fibrinolysis (normal)
Normal Range: <250 μg/mL (varies by assay)
Sensitivity: More sensitive than D-Dimer
DIC Indicator: Gold standard for DIC detection
Clinical Applications:
  • DIC detection (more sensitive than D-Dimer)
  • Detecting primary fibrinolysis
  • Monitoring fibrinolytic therapy (tPA)
  • Severe sepsis assessment
  • Obstetric emergencies (placental abruption)
  • Massive transfusion protocols

7️⃣ AT-III (Antithrombin III) Natural Anticoagulant

What It Measures: Activity of natural anticoagulant that inhibits thrombin and other factors - critical for heparin function
Biological Role:
AT-III (Natural Inhibitor) + Thrombin & Factors II, IX, X, XI, XII → Inactivates Them → Prevents Excess Clotting

Why AT-III Is Critical:

  • Heparin Effectiveness: HEPARIN REQUIRES AT-III TO WORK! Heparin enhances AT-III activity 1000-fold. Low AT-III = heparin resistance
  • Thrombosis Risk: Low AT-III (hereditary or acquired) = High thrombosis risk (blood clots too easily)
  • Natural Anticoagulation Reserve: Shows how much natural protection patient has against clotting
  • Sepsis Indicator: In severe sepsis, AT-III consumed rapidly → Low levels = Poor prognosis
Normal Range: 80-120% activity
Low AT-III Problem: Heparin won't work properly
Heparin Resistance: May need higher heparin doses
Clinical Applications:
  • Assessing heparin effectiveness
  • Detecting heparin resistance (low AT-III)
  • Identifying hereditary AT-III deficiency
  • Thrombosis risk assessment
  • Sepsis severity assessment
  • DIC monitoring (AT-III consumed)
