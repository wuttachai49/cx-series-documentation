Section 7.13 - Performing Mixing Tests: Complete Step-by-Step Explanation

Section 7.13 Overview: Performing Mixing Tests

What is a Mixing Test? (From Manual)

Official Definition: "Mixing test is a kind of screening test. They are usually performed when the clotting time of patient blood plasma un-explained lengthens. During mixing tests, patient plasma is mixed with normal plasma with specified ratio, and tested for clotting time again."

When to Perform Mixing Tests

  • Patient has unexplained prolonged clotting time (PT or APPT)
  • Need to determine if prolongation is due to factor deficiency or inhibitor
  • As follow-up test when routine coagulation screening is abnormal

The Clinical Logic

Key Principle: By mixing patient plasma with normal plasma at a specified ratio, the test answers: "Does mixing with normal plasma improve (correct) the patient's clotting time?"

  • If CORRECTS: Patient likely has factor deficiency (dilution with normal plasma adds missing factors)
  • If DOES NOT CORRECT: Patient likely has inhibitor (inhibitor attacks normal factors too)
Note from Manual: "For incubated mixing tests, the analyzer incubates the samples for the defined duration, and then start the test." - This is the CX-6000's capability for detecting time-dependent inhibitors.

Section 7.13.1: Preparing Normal Plasma

Critical Foundation of Mixing Study

The quality of normal plasma is CRITICAL to the validity of mixing test results. This is your reference standard - it must be reliable.

Option 1: Using Commercial Normal Plasma

From Manual: "When using commercial normal plasma, strictly follow the manufacturer's instruction to use and store the normal plasma."

  • Purchase from reputable reagent supplier
  • Follow product-specific instructions (each brand may differ)
  • Store according to manufacturer specifications
  • Check expiration date before use

Option 2: Preparing Your Own Normal Plasma (Pooled Plasma)

📋 Mandatory Requirements from Manual

To prepare normal plasma, ALL of these requirements must be met:

1️⃣ Sample Source

  • Minimum 20 samples from healthy people (recommended)
  • All donors must have NORMAL routine anticoagulation test results
  • Expected coagulation factor concentration: 80%-120% (normal range)

2️⃣ Sample Quality

  • NO hemolysis: Red blood cells must not be broken (hemoglobin interferes with optical measurement)
  • NO icterus: No yellow bilirubin discoloration (bilirubin absorbs at 405nm wavelength used by analyzer)
  • NO lipemia: No lipids/fat (particles scatter light, interfere with turbidimetric measurement)

3️⃣ Freshness

  • RECOMMENDED: Fresh normal plasma before each mixing test
  • If not fresh, must be frozen and properly handled (see next steps)

Handling Frozen Normal Plasma

Step-by-Step Thawing Procedure (From Manual)

1

Remove from Freezer

Take cryogenic vials (frozen tubes) out of storage

2

Thaw at 37°C Water Bath

Duration: 5 to 10 minutes

Wait until: Frozen sample completely thaws

Temperature: 37°C (body temperature - same as analyzer)

3

Mix the Sample

Method: Slightly invert (flip) the vials to well mix

Why: Ensure uniform distribution of coagulation factors after thawing

4

CRITICAL - Do NOT Refreeze!

From Manual: "Do not freeze and thaw the samples repeatedly."

Why: Repeated freeze-thaw cycles damage coagulation factors and destroy inhibitors, affecting test validity

Quality Control Before Using Thawed Normal Plasma

⚠️ Important Requirement from Manual

"When the normal plasma has been frozen and thawed, perform the routine anticoagulation tests on the plasma first. Perform the mixing tests only when the routine anticoagulation test results are OK."

What This Means:

  • Before using thawed normal plasma for mixing tests, you MUST run QC tests on it
  • Run PT, APPT, and Fibrinogen on the normal plasma itself
  • Verify all results are in NORMAL range
  • Only if QC passes → use for mixing tests
  • If QC fails → discard and prepare fresh plasma

Why This Quality Control?

  • Frozen thawing may have damaged factors
  • Contamination may have occurred
  • Verify plasma is suitable reference standard

Normal Plasma Troubleshooting

What to Do If Normal Plasma QC Fails

  • ❌ DO NOT use for mixing tests
  • ❌ DO NOT proceed with patient testing
  • ✅ Discard the thawed plasma properly (follow lab protocol)
  • ✅ Prepare fresh normal plasma or use new commercial product
  • ✅ Retest fresh/new plasma before proceeding

Section 7.13.2: Preparing Patient Samples

Standard Procedures Apply

From Manual: "Follow standard sample preparation procedures to prepare patient samples. Refer to 7.8 Preparing Samples."

The manual directs you to standard sample prep (section 7.8), which includes:

Patient Sample Requirements for Mixing Tests

Sample Collection

  • Collect in standard citrate tubes (3.2% sodium citrate)
  • Proper blood:citrate ratio (usually 9:1)
  • Standard venipuncture technique

Sample Quality

  • Minimal hemolysis (same requirement as normal plasma)
  • No lipemia or icterus
  • No clots in sample

Sample Handling

  • Room temperature processing (preferred)
  • Test within specified time frame (typically 4 hours)
  • Proper labeling and identification
  • Protect from light and contamination

Pre-Analysis Preparation

  • Centrifugation if needed (follow lab protocol)
  • Inspect for quality issues before testing
  • Ensure sample is at room temperature

Special Consideration for Mixing Tests

Why sample quality matters in mixing tests:

  • Hemolyzed patient sample → optical interference → invalid results
  • Lipemic or icteric patient sample → inaccurate measurement → wrong diagnosis
  • Patient sample with clots → analyzer blockage or sampling error
Clinical Tip: If patient sample appears hemolyzed, lipemic, or icteric, recollect before performing mixing study. The test must use clear, high-quality samples.

What CX-6000 Automation Does During Mixing Test

Behind-the-Scenes Analyzer Operations (Automatic)

Once you press "Start", the CX-6000 does all of this automatically:

Step 1: Patient Baseline Test

  • Aspirates patient sample from specified position
  • Transfers to reaction cuvette
  • Adds APPT reagent (partial thromboplastin)
  • Measures clotting time at 405nm absorbance
  • Result = APPT1 (patient baseline)

Step 2: Mix Preparation

  • Aspirates patient sample (portion)
  • Aspirates normal plasma (portion)
  • Mixes at exact 1:1 ratio (50% + 50%)
  • Precise automation = no human error

Step 3: Immediate Mix Test

  • Tests mixed plasma immediately (within minutes)
  • Adds APPT reagent to fresh mix
  • Measures clotting time
  • Results = APPT2, APPT3 (duplicate measurements)
  • Compares with APPT1 → calculates CORRECTION

Step 4: Incubation (If Configured)

  • Remaining mixed plasma placed in incubator
  • Temperature: 37°C (body temperature)
  • Time: Configurable (typically 30-120 minutes)
  • Awaits post-incubation testing

Step 5: Post-Incubation Test (If Enabled)

  • After incubation time completes
  • Tests incubated mixed plasma
  • Results = APPT6, APPT7 (duplicate post-incubation)
  • Compares with immediate mix → detects time-dependent effects

Step 6: Automatic Calculation & Interpretation

  • Calculates Correction Index (RI_CT)
  • Calculates Post-Incubation Index (RI_ICT)
  • Generates automatic diagnosis
  • Compares against predefined thresholds
  • Flags abnormal patterns

APPT Measurements Explained: APPT1 Through APPT7

The Complete Sequence of APPT Tests During Mixing Study

During a CX-6000 mixing study, the analyzer performs multiple APPT measurements at different stages. Each measurement has a specific purpose and name. Understanding what each one represents is critical for interpreting results correctly.

Key Principle: The numbering (APPT1, APPT2, etc.) represents the SEQUENCE and TIMING of tests, not the patient. All tests are on the SAME patient sample in different states (patient baseline, immediately after mixing, after incubation, etc.).

🔴 APPT1: Patient Baseline (Initial Test)

When it happens: FIRST test, at very beginning of mixing study

What it measures: Patient's own plasma APPT value WITHOUT any mixing

How it's done:

  • Analyzer aspirates 150 µL of pure patient plasma
  • Heats to 37°C
  • Adds APPT reagent (partial thromboplastin + calcium)
  • Measures time until fibrin formation detected at 405nm

Typical values:

  • Normal patient: 25-35 seconds
  • Prolonged (why mixing study ordered): > 40 seconds

Clinical meaning:

  • Confirms APPT is abnormally prolonged
  • Serves as reference baseline for all later comparisons
  • If APPT1 is normal but study ordered anyway: may indicate previous test error or patient condition changed

Example (Hemophilia A): Patient's APPT1 = 65 seconds (VERY prolonged - red flag for deficiency or inhibitor)

🟡 APPT2: Immediate Mix Test #1 (First Test After Mixing)

When it happens: Within 2-5 minutes after creating 1:1 mix of patient + normal plasma

What it measures: APPT of freshly mixed sample (immediate correction test)

How it's done:

  • Analyzer mixes exactly 50% patient plasma + 50% normal plasma (precise 1:1 ratio)
  • Tests the fresh mix immediately (no waiting, no incubation yet)
  • Adds APPT reagent to the mixed sample
  • Measures clotting time at 405nm

Typical values:

  • If corrects (deficiency): Returns to NORMAL range (25-35 sec)
  • If doesn't correct (inhibitor): Stays prolonged (>40 sec)

Clinical meaning:

  • First clue about whether patient has deficiency or inhibitor
  • If much shorter than APPT1 → suggests deficiency (mixing helps)
  • If similar to APPT1 → suggests inhibitor (mixing doesn't help)

Example (Hemophilia A): APPT2 = 35 seconds (improved from 65! → suggests deficiency)

Example (Lupus LA): APPT2 = 55 seconds (barely improved from 62 → suggests inhibitor)

🟡 APPT3: Immediate Mix Test #2 (Quality Control Duplicate)

When it happens: Immediately after APPT2 (within seconds)

What it measures: REPEAT of APPT2 (same mixed sample, tested again)

How it's done:

  • Tests the SAME fresh mix again
  • Ensures reproducibility of results
  • CV (coefficient of variation) should be < 5%

Why two tests (APPT2 and APPT3)?

  • Quality control - confirms APPT2 result wasn't a fluke
  • If APPT2 and APPT3 don't match closely → analyzer flags error
  • Professional standard: all coagulation tests done in duplicate

Typical values:

  • Should be VERY similar to APPT2 (within 1-2 seconds)
  • Example: APPT2 = 35 sec, APPT3 = 36 sec (excellent reproducibility)

Clinical meaning:

  • Confirms the immediate mix result is reliable
  • If APPT2 and APPT3 differ > 5%: investigate sample quality or analyzer issue

Used in calculations: Both APPT2 and APPT3 are averaged to calculate Correction Index (RI_CT)

⏱️ INCUBATION PERIOD (Between APPT3 and APPT6)

What happens: Remaining mixed plasma placed in incubator (NOT tested)

Duration: Configurable, typically 30-120 minutes at 37°C

Temperature: Exactly 37°C (body temperature) ± 0.2°C precision

Purpose: Allows time-dependent inhibitors to activate/develop

Why this is critical:

  • Factor VIII antibodies don't activate instantly
  • At room temperature: antibodies may be inactive
  • At 37°C for extended time: antibodies fully activate
  • This is why CX-9000's 120-minute incubation catches what 15-minute competitors miss!

No APPT tests during incubation:

  • Incubation period is WAITING period
  • Next tests (APPT6/7) happen AFTER incubation completes

🔵 APPT6: Post-Incubation Test #1 (After Incubation)

When it happens: AFTER incubation period completes (e.g., after 120 minutes at 37°C)

What it measures: APPT of the SAME mixed sample after it's been warmed and given time for inhibitor activation

How it's done:

  • Removes mixed plasma from incubator
  • Adds fresh APPT reagent
  • Tests the now-incubated mixed sample
  • Measures clotting time at 405nm

Typical values:

  • If NO time-dependent inhibitor: Similar to APPT2/3 (deficiency stays corrected)
  • If TIME-DEPENDENT INHIBITOR present: MUCH WORSE (longer) than APPT2/3 (correction is lost!)

Clinical meaning:

  • If APPT6 ≈ APPT2/3: No time-dependent effect, patient likely has simple factor deficiency
  • If APPT6 >> APPT2/3 (significantly worse): Time-dependent inhibitor detected! Antibodies developed during incubation and inactivated normal factors

Example (Hemophilia A with Developing Factor VIII Antibodies):

  • APPT1 (patient): 58 seconds
  • APPT2/3 (immediate mix): 38 seconds (looked corrected!)
  • APPT6 (after 120-min incubation): 48 seconds (WORSENED by 10 seconds!)
  • Diagnosis: TIME-DEPENDENT INHIBITOR (developing antibody)

🔵 APPT7: Post-Incubation Test #2 (Quality Control Duplicate)

When it happens: Immediately after APPT6

What it measures: REPEAT of APPT6 (same incubated sample, tested again)

How it's done:

  • Tests the SAME incubated mix again
  • Ensures reproducibility of post-incubation result
  • CV should be < 5%

Why two tests (APPT6 and APPT7)?

  • Quality control - confirms APPT6 result
  • If APPT6 and APPT7 don't match: error flag
  • Post-incubation samples may be more prone to variability

Typical values:

  • Should match APPT6 closely (within 1-2 seconds)
  • Example: APPT6 = 48 sec, APPT7 = 50 sec

Used in calculations: Both APPT6 and APPT7 averaged to calculate Post-Incubation Correction Index (RI_ICT)

Complete APPT1-7 Sequence - Official Definition Table

Test # Sample Composition Incubation Test Timing Purpose Expected Value (seconds)
APPT1 Patient Plasma (PP) alone None Immediately Patient baseline clotting time 25-35 sec (normal) or >40 sec (prolonged)
APPT2 Normal Pooled Plasma (NPP) alone None Immediately Normal plasma baseline QC 25-35 sec (must be normal range)
APPT3 PP + NPP 1:1 fresh mix None - test fresh Immediately after mixing Immediate correction test (RI_CT calculation) Normal IF corrects, prolonged IF doesn't correct
APPT4 Patient Plasma (PP) alone 37°C for 2 hours Test after incubation Time-dependent effect on patient sample May change if inhibitor is time-dependent
APPT5 Normal Pooled Plasma (NPP) alone 37°C for 2 hours Test after incubation Time-dependent effect on normal sample (reference) Should remain in normal range
APPT6 PP + NPP 1:1 THEN incubated 37°C for 2 hours AFTER mixing Test immediately after incubation Post-incubation correction (fresh mix then incubated) Changes if time-dependent inhibitor present
APPT7 Incubated PP + Incubated NPP 1:1 mixed 37°C for 2 hours SEPARATELY, then mixed Test immediately after mixing incubated samples Post-incubation correction (incubate then mix) Tests inhibitor development after separate incubation
🔑 Key Difference - APPT6 vs APPT7:
  • APPT6: Mix THEN incubate - tests the effect on mixed plasma
  • APPT7: Incubate THEN mix - tests the effect on individual samples that are incubated separately
  • Comparing APPT6 and APPT7 reveals different inhibitor activation patterns

Understanding RI_CT and RI_ICT - What Do These Abbreviations Mean?

📌 Abbreviation Breakdown

RI = Ratio Index (measures the ratio/percentage of correction)

CT = Correction Test (immediate testing with fresh mix)

ICT = Incubation Correction Test (testing after 37°C incubation)

🔍 RI_CT - Immediate Correction Test

What it measures: The percentage correction when fresh patient plasma is mixed 1:1 with normal plasma and tested IMMEDIATELY (no incubation)

Clinical purpose: Quick screening to detect immediate-acting inhibitors and factor deficiencies

Timing: Test within 2-5 minutes of mixing

What it tells you: Does mixing IMMEDIATELY improve the clotting time?

🔍 RI_ICT - Incubation Correction Test

What it measures: The percentage correction after the mixed plasma has been incubated at 37°C for 2 hours

Clinical purpose: Deep diagnosis to detect time-dependent inhibitors that develop during heating

Timing: Test AFTER 37°C/2-hour incubation

What it tells you: Does the correction PERSIST after heating, or does it WORSEN?

Official Calculation Formulas (From Mindray)

✅ Official Formulas from Mindray Presentation

These are the OFFICIAL formulas used by Mindray for the CX-9000 mixing study calculations:

Formula 1: RI_CT (Immediate Correction Index)

Calculation for APPT3 (Immediate Mix Test):

RI_CT = [(APPT3 - APPT2) / APPT1] × 100%

Where:

  • APPT1 = Patient plasma baseline (denominator/reference)
  • APPT2 = Normal plasma baseline (subtracted as reference)
  • APPT3 = Fresh 1:1 mix (numerator/test result)

Threshold: 11%

Interpretation:

  • RI_CT < 11%: Small correction → ✅ FACTOR DEFICIENCY
  • RI_CT > 11%: Large correction → ❌ INHIBITOR PRESENT

Formula 2: RI_ICT (Post-Incubation Correction Index)

Calculation for APPT6 (After Incubation - Mix Then Incubate):

RI_ICT = [(APPT6 - APPT5) / APPT4] × 100%

Where:

  • APPT4 = Incubated patient plasma (denominator/reference after heating)
  • APPT5 = Incubated normal plasma (subtracted as reference after heating)
  • APPT6 = Incubated 1:1 mix (numerator/test result after heating)

Threshold: 11%

Additional Check - Time-Temperature Dependency:

Δ APPT = APPT7 - APPT6
If Δ APPT > 3 seconds: Time-dependent inhibitor present

Interpretation:

  • RI_ICT < 11% & Δ APPT ≤ 3s: ✅ FACTOR DEFICIENCY (stays corrected)
  • RI_ICT > 11% & Δ APPT > 3s: 🔴 TIME-DEPENDENT INHIBITOR (worsens after incubation)
  • RI_ICT > 11% & Δ APPT ≤ 3s: ❌ NON-TIME-DEPENDENT INHIBITOR (immediate-acting)

Time-Dependent Assessment

Δ APPT = APPT6/7 - APPT2/3

What it measures: How much the result CHANGED after incubation

Interpretation:

  • Δ APPT < 3 seconds: NO time-dependent effect (normal pattern)
  • Δ APPT > 3 seconds WORSE: SIGNIFICANT time-dependent inhibitor (critical finding!)

Example: APPT2/3 = 38 sec, APPT6/7 = 50 sec → Δ APPT = 12 sec (much worse!) → TIME-DEPENDENT INHIBITOR DETECTED

Complete Example: All APPT Values in Real Case

Case: Hemophilia A Patient (Factor VIII Deficiency)

  • APPT1: 65 seconds (patient alone - very prolonged)
  • APPT2: 35 seconds (immediate mix test 1)
  • APPT3: 36 seconds (immediate mix test 2)
  • RI_CT: 46% (CORRECTS - > 11%)
  • Incubation: 120 minutes at 37°C
  • APPT6: 35 seconds (post-incubation test 1)
  • APPT7: 37 seconds (post-incubation test 2)
  • RI_ICT: 43% (STAYS CORRECTED - > 11%)
  • Δ APPT: 1 second (NO worsening - normal pattern)
  • FINAL DIAGNOSIS: ✅ FACTOR DEFICIENCY (Hemophilia A)

Case: Developing Factor VIII Inhibitor (Time-Dependent)

  • APPT1: 58 seconds (patient alone - prolonged)
  • APPT2: 38 seconds (immediate mix - LOOKS CORRECTED!)
  • APPT3: 39 seconds (immediate mix repeat)
  • RI_CT: 34% (CORRECTS - > 11%)
  • Incubation: 120 minutes at 37°C (antibodies activate!)
  • APPT6: 48 seconds (post-incubation - MUCH WORSE!)
  • APPT7: 50 seconds (post-incubation repeat)
  • RI_ICT: 14% (LOST CORRECTION - < 11%)
  • Δ APPT: 11 seconds (SIGNIFICANTLY WORSE after incubation)
  • FINAL DIAGNOSIS: 🔴 TIME-DEPENDENT INHIBITOR (Developing Factor VIII Antibody) - URGENT!

What The Analyzer Shows You

Test Result What It Means Clinical Interpretation

(Improvement but not complete)		 Partial improvement suggests mixed situation ⚠️ Combined Defect

Next Clinical Steps (After Results)

Based on Mixing Test Results, Physician Orders:

  • If Deficiency: Specific factor assays (Factor VIII, Factor IX, etc.) to quantify deficiency level
  • If Inhibitor: Specific inhibitor testing (Lupus LA, Factor inhibitor titer, etc.)
  • If Time-Dependent: URGENT inhibitor titer to quantify and guide therapy
  • If Combined: Both factor assay AND inhibitor testing

Key Takeaways from Section 7.13

  • Quality of normal plasma is critical: Use fresh, QC-verified samples
  • Proper sample placement is critical: Follow screen display exactly
